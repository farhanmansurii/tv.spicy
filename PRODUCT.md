# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Spicy TV is for people browsing and watching movies or TV shows from a web app, often in a relaxed entertainment context. Users arrive to find something worth watching, search for a known title, continue a partially watched item, or manage saved media across watchlist, favorites, and recently watched history.

The primary workflow is not reading marketing content. It is choosing and playing media with as little friction as possible.

## Product Purpose

Spicy TV helps users discover, inspect, save, and watch TMDB-backed movie and TV content. Success looks like a user quickly finding a title, trusting the detail page enough to press play, and returning later with their progress, watchlist, and favorites intact.

## Brand Personality

Cinematic calm, direct, and polished.

The interface should feel like a quiet streaming surface: dark, image-led, restrained, and confident. It should not compete with the posters and backdrops. The UI should support browsing, comparison, and playback without turning every section into a decorative showcase.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS dashboards with bright gradients, glass cards everywhere, and metric-heavy hero sections.
- Overly playful entertainment apps where novelty gets in the way of picking a title.
- Dense database UIs that treat films and shows like rows instead of visual media objects.
- Streaming pages that hide primary actions, bury seasons, or make playback state feel uncertain.
- AI-looking landing-page tropes: repeated uppercase eyebrows, oversized slogan copy, identical icon-card grids, neon glows, and decorative gradients.

## Design Principles

1. **Let the media carry the drama.** Posters, backdrops, logos, and motion stills should provide the emotional texture; controls and metadata should stay restrained.
2. **Make route state obvious.** Browse, discover, detail, playback, library, and profile surfaces should reveal where the user is and what can happen next.
3. **Prefer confident defaults.** Use familiar streaming patterns for rows, cards, tabs, search, and playback controls. Novelty belongs in moments, not core affordances.
4. **Keep persistence legible.** Watchlist, favorites, recently watched, and sync states should make saved/unsaved and local/synced behavior clear.
5. **Design for interruption.** Users may leave mid-episode, return later, switch devices, or recover from a failed provider. The UI should preserve context and provide clear recovery.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Accessibility is a pragmatic baseline rather than a brand-defining constraint, but the product should still respect core usability:

- Maintain readable contrast for text, controls, and metadata on dark surfaces.
- Preserve visible focus states for keyboard users.
- Keep touch targets large enough for mobile browsing.
- Avoid hiding content behind motion; reduced-motion users should get stable, non-blank content.
- Do not rely on color alone for saved, active, loading, error, or unavailable states.
